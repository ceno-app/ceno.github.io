What is CENO?

CENO is the world’s first mobile browser that circumvents current Internet censorship methods and local Internet outages using peer-to-peer (p2p) technology. Designed to allow people anywhere in the world to access web content in censored or Internet-restricted regions - and in countries where connectivity is unreliable or costly - it can be used to create bridges for others to access censored or restricted content.

How CENO Browser Works

Most web traffic in countries that filter internet is routed through centralized exchanges, such as Google.

This design gives the censor an advantage in locating and blocking popular proxies, VPN servers, relays, etc;

In these cases, reaching origin web servers is difficult or impossible, and your device will not be able to get that content.

CENO is a mobile web browser for storing and sharing web content anywhere.

CENO users create small-world networks to connect to each other, and thereafter to a bridge outside the censored zone.

Once a website is accessed by a single CENO user, it is stored and shared inside the country peer-to-peer.

Who is it for?

Journalists

She works in a liberal opposition news media as a reporter, has some digital security training and is aware of risks and existing tools, but still has a rather patchworked and unstable online security pattern. Uses a VPN recommended by the media they work for, but still looks for other options. Needs circumvention tools almost every day for work (mostly to access blocked media websites and collect data for their own articles). Appreciates speed and reliability of the tool. Appreciates a lot if the tool also works on desktop, since her work is about writing texts and collecting / analyzing data, and a desktop version could provide more convenience.

Human Rights Observers

An activist working in a NGO specialized in defending freedom of assembly and supporting political prisoners (providing legal help, crowdfunding for lawyer’s fees, informing citizens about their rights, gathering testimonies of police violence and collecting statistics of arrests during protests). He or she has regular digital security trainings, and a rather advanced organizational security policy, but still is not 100 % perfect in their online practices. Uses Telegram, but also Signal, tried other messaging apps (keeps at least 4 of them on their phone) ; uses Tor, but is concerned by the reputation of the tool (main threat model : device seizure during protests / arrests). User 3 needs a good circumvention tool that he or she can access from a mobile phone when he or she is in mobility (during a street demonstration, for example). Reliability is a central feature, since Internet shutdowns or slowdowns are frequent during such events.

Student Activists

A member of a student union / NGO focused on student rights. He or she is an active social media user. Organizes actions, performances, launches petitions. Actively uses Instagram, Youtube and Facebook to communicate about the Union’s activities, but also uses encrypted messengers such as Signal or Telegram to prepare actions / transfer more sensitive data. Is interested in decentralization in principle (heard about Mastodon, uses Tor sometimes…). The website of their student organization has recently been blocked by the RosKomNadzor, so they use VPN almost every day to access the resource. Needs a tool that works both on mobile and desktop. Overloaded with work and studies, has too many different tools, and is skeptical about installing a new one, only if it is somewhat multi-functional.

Internet Freedom Defenders

Works in IT (as developer, sysadmin, network administrator, designer, devOps…)and is concerned about the state of online censorship around the world. He or she has advanced knowledge of how Internet works, and likes to try different privacy enhancing tools (actually, reads a lot about new developments in the field of privacy enhancing technologies). Is a defender of open source and free software. Likes « old school » technologies such as Jabber or IRC, is interested in mesh networking and decentralized/distributed networks (in principle, as architectural solutions, but also for privacy and reliability reasons). Looks for a new distributed circumvention solution that can become an alternative to VPN / Tor. Is skeptical though, needs to have a look at the code or to read someone’s detailed review/audit before actually trusting a tool. Uses Linux and Android, appreciates when apps are available on Fdroid or apks are available directly from GitHub / GitLab. Appreciates even more when builds are available for Linux. Is ready to participate in a project if he/she likes it (either as a « seeder » or a node in the system to help distribute content ; or as a bug reporter).

Expats

This person has moved outside of the country, but is invested in communicating and sharing information with friends and family back home. They are fairly tech-savvy and interested in serving as a potential bridge node, expanding the network and accessibility of content.

Everybody!

Keep CENO on your device as a backup tool for accessing online content in the event of censorship or natural disaster.
